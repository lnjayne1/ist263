Introduction to Emergency Food Systems:

The term “food assistance” and “emergency food systems” refer to resources employed to support food access among food insecure individuals with the intention of fostering economic mobility. These programs operate on the federal, state, and local/private levels. The federal programs are enshrined in law, and the budgets are determined every 5 years as a part of the US Farm Bill. Programs like SNAP, Free Breakfast, and WIC are only available to US citizens and orchestrated through state offices with federal dollars. State and local programs can include food pantries, food banks, and food recovery which can either be publicly or privately funded. This page will elaborate on each of these programs and recognize some shortcomings. It is important to recognize that many food assistance efforts currently in place do very little to address root causes of food insecurity. These efforts can be very critical to filling the nutritive gaps left by those systemic barriers to food access, but they struggle to meet the ever growing number demand of people requesting these resources.

Federal Programs:

Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP):

The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (formerly known as food stamps) is directed by the USDA. It offers a monthly allowance to families to spend at participating food retailers to feed their household. Eligibility for SNAP is determined by household income, and individuals must register with their state office. SNAP benefits are available on an Electronic Benefit Transfer card (EBT) that acts like a debit card. This money can be spent on fresh produce, meats, dairy products, breads/cereals, snack foods, and seeds/plants. Hot foods, alcohol, drugs, cleaning supplies, and pet food are not covered by SNAP.

Limitations:

  • SNAP benefits are often not enough to cover the prices of a varied, robust diet
  • Not available to felons or undocumented US residents
  • Applying for SNAP has high stigma around it and can be a confusing process
  • People lacking ability to space to prepare food can't by ready to eat food

Free/Reduced Meal Programs:

The National School Lunch Program has been offering free and reduced price meals in public and private schools across the country since 1975. The Black Panther Party actually first pioneered a Free Breakfast Program in the 1960s in Oakland, CA which was quickly adopted by the federal government. State agencies run this program in collaboration with local school districts. Meals offered are required to meet Federal nutrition standards. Revenue lost by offering free and reduced cost meals is reimbursed by the USDA. Children from families with incomes under 185% of the federal poverty level are eligible for the program. Access to meals in school is critical for supporting prolonged focus, good mood, and strong academic performance.

Limitations:

  • Children are shameful or bullied for using this resource and may forego meals
  • Foods are prepared at extremely low cost sacrificing taste and local preferences
  • Families who are ineligible for the program may still stuggle to prepare daily lunches for their children

Special SNAP for Women, Infants, and Children:

The Special Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program for Women, Infants, and Children expands the benefits of SNAP to include particular needs of mothers and their young children. Federal grants support the purchasing of supplemental foods, health care referrals, and nutrition education for low-income, pregnant, and non-breastfeeding postpartum women, infants, and children under age of 5 who are at nutritional risk. Authorized foods include baby foods, formula, medical foods, in addition to the foods allowed by SNAP. Applicants must be found to be at nutrition risk to be eligible. Nutrition risk is identified as anemia, underweight, history of pregnancy complications, and failure to meet current dietary guidelines.

Limitations:

  • Low variation in financial packages despite high variation of need presented in those receiving benefits
  • No provisions for scheduling and transportation required to access benefits
  • Participation across the country varies dramatically. Portion of the population who are eligible is growing steadily, but enrollment is not.

State and Locally Run Programs:

Food Banks

Regionally Run with Federal Support:

Food banks are store houses for donated, purchased, and federally financed food that is distributed across some area to food pantries. Not to be confused with food pantries, food banks are more like a warehouse that collects the food rather than directly gives it to people who need it. Food Banks get public funding to host a variety of other programs like nutrition education, SNAP/public assistance registration, and budgeting workshops. Some food banks may even have a food policy council affiliated with it. These groups bring together stakeholders across the community to establish goals that support community food access.

Limitations:

  • May be unaware of the tastes and preferences of local pantries
  • Criticized for not using enough resources to advocate for structural reform
  • Commodity food (shelf stable, surplus food) offered through The Emergency Food Assistance Program supports consolidated agriculture interests

Food Pantry:

Regionally and Privately run with Federal support

Food pantries and organizations like soup kitchens are the most common types of food assistance we interact with. These services are typically run by non profit, religious institutions, but there are a few publically-run pantries across the country. Pantries function by collecting donations of food and redistributing products to their patrons. Pantries can also go mobile to serve people who may not otherwise have transportation to the brick and mortar location. Either way, food is typically distributed weekly and donations from local farms and community members can ensure that the meals cover all 5 food groups to curate a nutritious meal.

Limitations:

  • Ultra-processed, high sodium, or out of date food is often donated through food drives to be distributed to vulnerable populations
  • Aging infrastructure of the physical institutions present high cost of maintenance that covered by donations and grants
  • Intended to be a temporary resource, but most patrons regularly use this for weekly food allowance

Food Recovery/Gleaning:

Regionally and Privately Directed

Leftover food from grocers, dining facilities, and restaurants are often thrown out at the end of the day to preserve food safety. There are many volunteer efforts, however, to take this food from the source to distribute among communities in need. Recovered food can go to food banks, low-cost food retailers, or directly to community support agencies. This is a great method of reducing food waste and hunger, but is criticized for ignoring the preferences and dignity of the recipients

Limitations:

  • Food donations can be unreliable, and little choice is offered to patrons receiving food
  • Stigma associated with consuming recovered food hurts public image of these programs
  • Volunteer Labor often required which may limit reliability or growth potential

Can Current Food Assistance Efforts End Hunger in the US?

These are just a few programs that work to limit the impacts of food insecurity. It is important to recognize that these programs do not actively dismantle the causes of food security. While it is important to have resources that fill in the gaps, leaving these systematic barriers to food access unaddressed will simply perpetuate the growth of the population who needs these resources. The term “emergency food system” has become a bit inappropriate as people access these resources regularly because they are stuck in poverty.
